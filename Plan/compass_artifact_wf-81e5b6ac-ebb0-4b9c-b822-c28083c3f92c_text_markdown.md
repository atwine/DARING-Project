# Evidence-based guidance for MICE in clinical prediction modeling

Modern statistical guidance on multiple imputation has evolved dramatically from classical recommendations, requiring **at least 20-50 imputations** rather than 3-5, with the number increasing quadratically based on the fraction of missing information. For internal validation, the **Boot-MI approach** (bootstrap first, then impute) provides the most robust methodology, addressing both sampling and imputation uncertainty while remaining valid under model misspecification. The TRIPOD+AI statement (2024) provides harmonized reporting standards emphasizing transparent documentation of missing data handling, integration of imputation within cross-validation frameworks, and separate strategies for model deployment. These methodological advances, established through rigorous simulation studies by White, von Hippel, Bartlett, Schomaker, and others, represent a fundamental shift toward more reproducible and statistically valid clinical prediction research.

The consensus emerges from three decades of methodological research showing that earlier recommendations prioritized computational efficiency over statistical validity, while contemporary standards emphasize reproducibility of inference, adequate statistical power, and proper uncertainty quantification. Van Buuren's foundational work on flexible imputation provides the practical framework, while recent validation studies establish that methodological choices about combining imputation with resampling procedures critically affect bias, coverage, and reproducibility of performance estimates.

## Determining the optimal number of imputations requires understanding missing information, not just missing values

The evolution from Rubin's classical recommendation of 3-5 imputations to modern guidance of 20-100 or more reflects fundamental reconceptualization of what multiple imputation aims to achieve. Rubin's 1987 recommendation focused narrowly on **relative efficiency of point estimates**, showing that with 30% missing information and 5 imputations, variance increases only 6% compared to infinite imputations. This efficiency-focused perspective dominated through the 1990s, with Schafer and colleagues reinforcing that "3-5 imputations are sufficient to obtain excellent results" in many applications.

Bodner's 2008 paper fundamentally challenged this optimism by demonstrating that **p-values, confidence interval widths, and estimates of the fraction of missing information suffer substantial imprecision** with small m. Using simulation studies, Bodner showed that researchers could draw "categorically different conclusions" about hypothesis tests across repeated MI runs when using few imputations. His empirical work established that with 30% missing information, 24 imputations are needed to ensure 95% of confidence interval widths fall within 10% of true values, while 50% missing information requires 59 imputations. This represented a fundamental shift from efficiency to reproducibility as the guiding principle.

White, Royston, and Wood's 2011 paper in Statistics in Medicine established what has become the **de facto standard in medical applications**: the number of imputations should equal at least the percentage of incomplete cases. Their reasoning focused on Monte Carlo error in inferential quantities rather than point estimates. When m approximates 100λ (where λ is the proportion of incomplete cases), the Monte Carlo error of the estimated coefficient is approximately 10% of its standard error, Monte Carlo error of test statistics is approximately 0.1, and Monte Carlo error of p-values is about 0.01 when the true p-value is 0.05. This rule provides practical balance between computational burden and statistical validity specifically for medical researchers who need reproducible results for publication.

Von Hippel's 2018-2020 work introduced the **quadratic rule**, demonstrating that the relationship between fraction of missing information (FMI) and required imputations is quadratic rather than linear: m = 1 + (1/2) × (γ_fmi / CV(SE))². The quadratic relationship explains why linear rules overestimate needs when FMI is below 0.5 but dangerously underestimate requirements when FMI exceeds 0.5. With FMI of 0.7, the linear rule suggests 70 imputations while the quadratic rule indicates 99 are needed; at FMI of 0.9, the gap widens from 90 to 163 imputations. Von Hippel recommends a **two-stage procedure**: conduct pilot analysis with moderate m (approximately 20) to obtain an FMI estimate with confidence interval, then use the upper bound of that interval to calculate final m via the quadratic rule, ensuring only 2.5% chance of under-imputing.

Graham and colleagues' 2007 work revealed that **statistical power for detecting small effects diminishes much more than relative efficiency predicts**. With 30% missing information and only 5 imputations, attained power was 73.1% versus theoretical 78.4%, but increasing to 20 imputations recovered essentially theoretical power at 78.2%. This matters critically for prevention science and clinical trials where detecting small but meaningful effects is paramount. The finding emphasizes that standard errors and p-values vary substantially with m, not at the same rate as relative efficiency, requiring "many more imputations than previously considered sufficient" for adequate power.

The critical distinction that emerges from this literature is that **fraction of missing information differs fundamentally from fraction of missing values**. FMI represents the proportion of total variance attributable to missing data rather than the simple percentage of cells that are empty. When predictors strongly correlate with incomplete variables, missing values can be well-imputed, yielding low FMI despite high missingness percentages. Von Hippel provides an example where 76% of values are missing but FMI remains only 0.38 due to strong predictive relationships. FMI depends on missingness pattern, strength of relationships with observed variables, number and quality of auxiliary variables, and analysis model specification. Since FMI is estimated rather than directly observed, and these estimates themselves are unstable with small m, the two-stage procedure or conservative rules based on incomplete case percentages provide more reliable guidance.

For clinical prediction models specifically, contemporary recommendations converge on **50-100 imputations for development** with similar requirements for validation. Sullivan and colleagues' 2018 work on randomized controlled trials and Deforth's 2024 prognostic model simulations both employed m=100, following White's rule that m should equal percentage of missing data. Van Buuren's 2018 book recommends 5-10 imputations during exploratory model building but 20-100 for final analysis, with the specific value matching average percentage of missing data. The range of 20-100 generally suffices for moderate missingness, but very high missingness (over 50% incomplete cases) may require 100-200+ imputations to ensure adequate reproducibility.

## Bootstrap-first methodology provides valid internal validation while properly accounting for uncertainty

The fundamental methodological question when combining multiple imputation with bootstrap validation concerns **the order of operations**: whether to impute the complete dataset then bootstrap (MI-Boot), or bootstrap the incomplete data then impute each sample (Boot-MI). Extensive simulation studies by Schomaker and Heumann (2018), Bartlett and Hughes (2020), and Wahl and colleagues (2016) establish that Boot-MI generally performs better and remains valid under broader conditions, particularly when imputation and analysis models are uncongenial (misspecified relative to each other).

Schomaker and Heumann's 2018 paper in Statistics in Medicine provides the definitive theoretical and empirical evidence. They compared four main approaches through rigorous simulation: MI-Boot uses Rubin's rules to combine bootstrap estimates across imputations; MI-Boot with pooled sample creates percentile confidence intervals from all B×M estimates; Boot-MI with pooled sample similarly pools but reverses the order; and **Boot-MI (recommended)** bootstraps incomplete data B times, applies MI to each bootstrap sample M times, averages the M estimates within each bootstrap using Rubin's rules, then uses the B averaged estimates for percentile confidence intervals. The Boot-MI approach properly separates sampling variability (addressed by bootstrapping) from imputation uncertainty (addressed by averaging within each bootstrap).

Bartlett and Hughes' 2020 work demonstrated that **only Boot-MI provides valid inference under uncongeniality**, the common situation where the imputation model and analysis model differ in specification. MI-Boot produces optimistically biased estimates with coverage probability dropping to 89-95% depending on missingness severity, because the imputation model "sees" information from the full dataset that wouldn't be available when applying the model to new data. Boot-MI maintains valid inference because each bootstrap sample is imputed independently, mirroring the real-world scenario where imputation and model fitting both occur on the training data. This theoretical advantage translates to practical superiority across diverse simulation conditions.

The specific Boot-MI procedure that emerges as best practice involves drawing B bootstrap samples (typically 200-500) from the incomplete original data, applying multiple imputation separately to each bootstrap sample generating M imputations (typically 10-50 per bootstrap), fitting the prediction model to each of the M imputed versions and calculating performance metrics, averaging the M performance estimates within each bootstrap using Rubin's rules, and constructing percentile confidence intervals from the B averaged estimates. This approach **requires B×M total model fits** but properly accounts for both sources of uncertainty while preventing data leakage between training and validation sets.

Wahl and colleagues' 2016 systematic comparison in BMC Medical Research Methodology introduced helpful terminology distinguishing Val-MI (perform bootstrap or cross-validation split, then impute training and test sets separately) from MI-Val (impute full dataset, then perform resampling) and MI(-y)-Val (impute without outcome, then resample). Their simulations showed Val-MI with bootstrap 0.632+ estimator provides largely unbiased estimates of optimism-corrected performance. The key insight is that **Val-MI prioritizes maximizing B over M**: SE decreases with √B in the Val-MI approach, making 500 bootstraps with 20 imputations superior to 200 bootstraps with 50 imputations for equivalent computational cost.

Optimism correction with multiple imputation requires careful integration of Harrell's bootstrap optimism framework with pooling procedures. For each bootstrap sample, the apparent performance (evaluated on bootstrap data) and test performance (evaluated on out-of-bag data) must both account for imputation uncertainty by averaging across M imputations before calculating optimism for that bootstrap replicate. The final optimism estimate averages across all B bootstrap replicates, and the optimism-corrected performance subtracts this mean optimism from the apparent performance calculated on imputed full data. The 0.632+ bootstrap estimator provides sophisticated weighted averaging that generally outperforms simple optimism correction by accounting for the relative overfitting rate.

Pooling performance metrics across imputations presents special challenges because many metrics are bounded or have non-normal distributions. For regression coefficients and similar unbounded parameters, **Rubin's rules apply directly**: the pooled estimate equals the average across imputations, while total variance combines within-imputation variance with between-imputation variance multiplied by (1 + 1/M). For the C-statistic, however, the [0.5, 1.0] bounds violate Rubin's rules assumptions. Debray and colleagues' 2016 work on meta-analysis of prediction models recommends **logit transformation**: transform each C_m using logit(C_m) = log[(C_m - 0.5)/(1 - C_m + 0.5)], apply Rubin's rules to the transformed values, then back-transform the pooled estimate and confidence bounds. Marshall's 2009 guidance suggests direct averaging often proves acceptable for performance measures in practice, with bootstrap providing valid standard error estimates.

The critical metrics for assessing robustness across imputations include between-imputation variance B, relative increase in variance (RIV = [(1 + 1/M)B]/W̄), and fraction of missing information (FMI). High between-imputation variance indicates substantial imputation uncertainty and possible need for more imputations or imputation model refinement. FMI values below 0.20 indicate modest impact, 0.20-0.40 represents moderate impact, while values exceeding 0.50 suggest high severity where inferences highly depend on imputation method choices. For variable selection stability, Heymans and colleagues' 2007 approach calculates **inclusion frequency** across B×M model fits, retaining variables selected in more than 50-70% of iterations, providing more stable final models than single-dataset selection.

## TRIPOD+AI requirements and best-practice pipelines emphasize reproducible workflows

The TRIPOD+AI statement published in April 2024 supersedes the original 2015 TRIPOD guidelines, providing harmonized guidance for both regression and machine learning approaches to clinical prediction. Collins, Moons, and colleagues expanded from 22 main items to 27 items (52 subitems), developed through two Delphi rounds with 170-200 participants from 22-27 countries. The statement emphasizes that **TRIPOD 2015 should no longer be used**, with TRIPOD+AI representing current best practice for all prediction model studies regardless of methodology.

Item 11 of TRIPOD+AI addresses missing data reporting: "Describe how missing data were handled. Provide reasons for omitting any data." The Explanation and Elaboration document specifies that complete reporting requires describing the amount of missingness (percentage of complete cases, proportion missing by variable, number of participants affected), nature of missingness (assessment of MCAR/MAR/MNAR mechanisms, possible reasons, differences across sociodemographic groups), handling methods (complete description of imputation methodology including number of imputations, imputation model details, variables included, handling of non-normal distributions, software used), and validation approach (how missing data handled during internal/external validation, comparison of imputed versus observed distributions).

Additional ML-specific requirements appear throughout the checklist with particular relevance to missing data handling. **Item 7 on data preparation** requires describing preprocessing and quality checking, including fairness across sociodemographic groups, addressing data cleaning before imputation. Item 12c on analytical methods must specify how imputation integrates with hyperparameter tuning and whether imputation occurs within or outside cross-validation—a critical methodological choice. Item 14 on fairness requires describing approaches to assess and address model fairness, including evaluation of whether missingness patterns differ by protected groups and how imputation affects different subgroups. Item 18f mandates code sharing including imputation code for full reproducibility. Item 27a on usability critically requires describing "how poor quality or unavailable input data should be assessed and handled when implementing the prediction model," recognizing that deployment-time missing data handling differs fundamentally from development-time imputation.

Nijman and colleagues' 2022 systematic review of 152 machine learning prediction model studies published in 2018-2019 revealed persistent poor practices: **37% reported nothing about missing data**, only 8% used multiple imputation, while 43% used complete-case analysis. This stands in stark contrast to improved practices in traditional statistical modeling following TRIPOD's initial publication, where multiple imputation usage increased from 38% to 50%. The discrepancy highlights that machine learning communities have been slower to adopt principled missing data methods, often relying on algorithms' built-in capabilities (like tree-based surrogate splits) without considering statistical properties or documentation requirements.

The best-practice pipeline architecture that emerges from methodological literature emphasizes **imputation within cross-validation** as the fundamental principle. Each cross-validation fold must fit the imputation model exclusively on its training data and apply that model to both training and validation portions. This prevents data leakage where information from test data influences training through imputation parameters. Implementation requires careful pipeline design, readily achievable in R using the mice package with caret or tidymodels frameworks, or in Python using sklearn's Pipeline with IterativeImputer, though Python support remains less mature than R.

The complete workflow proceeds through data quality assessment (missingness pattern analysis, MCAR/MAR/MNAR evaluation, visualization), imputation setup (defining per-variable imputation models, specifying predictor matrix, setting iterations and imputations, including outcome for MAR assumption), cross-validation with imputation (for each fold, fitting imputation on training data only, generating M imputations, training ML model with hyperparameter tuning via nested CV within each imputation, predicting on validation fold, pooling predictions), final model development (imputing full dataset M times, training final model on each, saving models or pooled model, documenting specifications), and deployment strategy (defining handling of missing values in new data through pattern submodels, single-observation imputation, or marginalization approaches).

Hoogland and colleagues' 2020 paper in Statistics in Medicine addresses the critical distinction between validation and deployment missing data handling. During development and validation, the outcome is available and should be included in imputation models to reduce bias under MAR. At deployment for individual patients, however, the outcome is unknown by definition. Three main approaches exist for deployment: **pattern submodels** fit separate models for each missing data pattern encountered during development and apply the appropriate model based on observed predictors for new patients; marginalization integrates over missing predictors using their conditional distributions; and single-observation FCS applies the chained equations algorithm to individual patients. Pattern submodels provide the most practical approach for clinical implementation, though they require sufficient sample sizes for each pattern during development.

Machine learning-enhanced multiple imputation methods like SuperMICE (Loh 2021) and MLMI represent recent innovations integrating ensemble learning with imputation. SuperMICE employs Super Learner algorithms for imputation models rather than traditional parametric methods, providing data-adaptive selection that reduces bias when relationships are complex or nonlinear. MLMI uses support vector machines, random forests, XGBoost, or penalized regression for imputation, often outperforming traditional parametric MICE when interactions and nonlinearities are present. These methods maintain the multiple imputation framework's theoretical properties while leveraging ML algorithms' flexibility for the imputation models themselves.

Van Buuren's foundational work provides detailed implementation guidance emphasizing that using "every bit of available information yields multiple imputations that have minimal bias and maximal efficiency." His four-step predictor selection strategy includes all complete-data model variables (predictors, outcome, interactions—with outcome inclusion being essential despite seeming counterintuitive), variables related to nonresponse (factors influencing missingness, stratification variables), variables explaining variance (highly correlated variables, auxiliary variables improving precision), while removing variables with excessive missingness. For most applications, 15-25 predictors suffice, selected using correlation thresholds and proportion of usable cases.

Predictive mean matching (PMM) emerges as the **gold standard imputation method** for continuous variables, preserving distributional properties including skewness and bounds without requiring transformations, using donor pools of 5 nearby observed values to ensure imputations are actual observed values. For binary variables, logistic regression provides the default, while ordered categorical variables use proportional odds models and unordered categorical use polytomous regression. Passive imputation maintains relationships for derived variables like BMI = weight/height², specifying the formula rather than imputing independently. Convergence requires 5-20 iterations depending on complexity, always verified through trace plots showing mean and standard deviation of imputed values stabilizing without trends, with streams from different imputations intermingling freely.

## Methodological synthesis and practical implementation recommendations

The synthesis of three decades of methodological research establishes clear evidence-based guidance for clinical researchers conducting prediction modeling with missing data under TRIPOD requirements. The fundamental shift from computational convenience to statistical validity manifests in every aspect: number of imputations increasing from 3-5 to 20-100, recognition that Boot-MI provides superior validity over MI-Boot, emphasis on reproducibility over efficiency, and integration of fairness and transparency throughout the pipeline.

For determining number of imputations in clinical datasets with missing vital signs, follow White's rule as the **practical starting point**: use at least as many imputations as the percentage of incomplete cases, so 30% incomplete cases requires minimum 30 imputations. For final published analyses, use 50-100 imputations as standard practice. If computational resources permit and missingness exceeds 30%, implement von Hippel's two-stage procedure: conduct pilot analysis with 20 imputations to estimate FMI with 95% confidence interval, use the upper confidence bound in the quadratic formula m = 200 × FMI² (for CV(SE) = 0.05), and conduct final analysis with calculated m. This adaptive approach optimally balances validity and efficiency.

For internal validation implementing bootstrapping with TRIPOD compliance, use the Boot-MI approach with 200-500 bootstrap samples (preferably 500) and 20-50 imputations per bootstrap (preferably matching percentage incomplete). Draw bootstrap samples from incomplete original data, impute each bootstrap sample separately, fit models to each imputation, average performance metrics within bootstrap using Rubin's rules (with logit transformation for C-statistic), calculate optimism as difference between bootstrap and out-of-bag performance, apply 0.632+ weighting for final optimism-corrected estimate, and construct 95% confidence intervals from percentile method across the B optimism-corrected estimates. Report the C-statistic with confidence interval, calibration slope and intercept with confidence intervals, optimism estimate, number of imputations and bootstraps, FMI and between-imputation variance, and interpretation accounting for both uncertainty sources.

For building statistically rigorous ML pipelines, implement imputation within cross-validation as the inviolable principle. Each fold must fit imputation models exclusively on fold training data and apply to both training and validation portions. Generate 20-50 imputations per fold, train ML models on each with nested cross-validation for hyperparameter tuning, pool predictions across imputations by averaging (for probabilistic outputs) or using Rubin's rules (for model parameters), and aggregate performance across folds using percentile methods that account for both sampling and imputation variability. Include all analysis model variables plus 10-15 auxiliary variables in imputation models, always including the outcome. Use PMM for continuous predictors, logistic for binary, proportional odds for ordered categorical. Check convergence with trace plots ensuring stabilization by 10-20 iterations. Validate imputed values fall within physiological ranges for vital signs and preserve expected relationships between correlated measures.

For TRIPOD+AI reporting, complete all items in the checklist with particular attention to Item 11 on missing data. Report percentages missing overall and by variable in a table, describe assessment of missing data mechanisms with evidence, specify number of imputations with justification based on White's rule or FMI, detail all imputation model specifications including method by variable type, list all predictor variables used in imputation with rationale, describe convergence diagnostics performed, explain how imputation integrated with cross-validation, provide code implementing the full pipeline, describe sensitivity analyses comparing methods, and address fairness by evaluating whether missingness differs across subgroups. In supplementary materials, include trace plots demonstrating convergence, density plots comparing observed and imputed distributions, detailed specifications of imputation models, and complete reproducible code.

The deployment strategy requires explicit planning distinct from development. Since outcome is unavailable for new patients, pattern submodels provide the most practical approach: identify common missing data patterns during development, fit separate final models for each pattern using only available predictors in that pattern, ensure adequate sample sizes for each pattern, validate performance within each pattern, and document which model applies for each possible pattern in deployment documentation. Alternative approaches include single-observation MICE (computationally intensive but theoretically sound) or marginalization (integrating over missing predictor distributions, complex to implement).

Vital signs present special considerations due to temporal structure and informative missingness. For cross-sectional analysis using admission vitals, standard MICE with physiological constraints suffices. For longitudinal monitoring data with gaps under one hour, linear interpolation often outperforms MI with lower error. For irregular intervals with large gaps, use MICE incorporating temporal predictors (lagged values, time since last measurement, measurement frequency) and patient characteristics. Consider time-dependent iterative imputation methods specifically developed for clinical time series. Always validate that imputed vital signs preserve expected physiological relationships (MAP ≈ DBP + (SBP-DBP)/3, respiratory rate correlations with SpO2, heart rate relationships with blood pressure) and fall within plausible ranges by age and clinical context.

The methodological consensus from leading statisticians—White, von Hippel, Bodner, Graham, Schomaker, Bartlett, Steyerberg, Marshall, Collins, Moons, van Buuren—provides clear evidence-based guidance superseding earlier convenience-based recommendations. Modern computational capabilities remove barriers that once justified minimizing imputations, while reproducibility crises in science demand more rigorous approaches. Clinical prediction modeling, given its direct impact on patient care, particularly requires adherence to these methodological standards, ensuring that missing data handling neither optimistically biases performance estimates nor pessimistically discards valuable information. The integration of principled statistical methods with machine learning approaches, transparent reporting under TRIPOD+AI, and practical implementation through well-designed pipelines represents the current state of the art for reproducible, valid clinical prediction research.
